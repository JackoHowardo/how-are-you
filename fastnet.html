Fastnet

A self-set proposal to turn an automated lighthouse into a home for four artists.

Fastnet is a small slate-rock island off the coast of West Cork — once a manned lighthouse, now fully automated and empty most of the year. METAMORPHOSIS was a studio project imagining it reoccupied: an artist residency built into the existing outbuilding, with room for up to four artists to live, work and perform.

The brief I set myself was transformation. A modular room, built from bespoke roll-up walls and pegboard partitions, converts between up to four private bedrooms and a single performance space — where visiting audiences can watch the work made on the island performed against a backdrop of open Atlantic.

I never got out to the rock itself. The whole design was built from plans Irish Lights kindly shared with me, which is part of why the report itself is so paper-based, ruler-scans and all.

The snap window

The building sits closer to the island's west side, which takes the worst of the Atlantic. I needed a window that could survive direct wave impact without sealing the room off from the view that's the whole point of being there.

I looked at how boats handle the same problem, since they deal with wave impact on glazing constantly. The result is a recessed frame in black brushed stainless, glazed with double-glazed polycarbonate — the same material used on boats for its strength and heat retention. The window opens outward on two centre hinges, so air moves up from the bottom and out the top, but a wave hitting it while open pushes it shut rather than tearing it off. The surrounding wall tapers to a conical point above the frame, forcing the wave up and back on itself instead of straight at the glass, and slopes at the base so water runs off rather than pooling.

Other problems solved

People kept getting in each other's way in the kitchen

Problem: cooking together in one shared space meant constant collisions. Approach: thought through how people actually move and bump into each other at a counter. Result: the kitchen island splits into four separate stations, each with its own mini fridge and chopping area, arranged around a shared central sink with taps that rotate to either side.

The lighthouse itself wasn't big enough

Problem: the original plan was to redesign the lighthouse interior. Approach: tested the actual floor space against everything the brief needed to fit. Result: realised there wasn't room, and moved the whole project onto the adjoining outbuilding instead, which became the real residence.

Heat was escaping straight up the stairwell

Problem: the open spiral stairwell is the lighthouse's largest volume, and heat was venting through it to the lantern. Approach: traced heat loss room by room against every door and opening. Result: sealed doors on every room off the stairwell, plus a hatch near the lantern to stop heat escaping out the top.

Rain sitting on the slate-inspired windows

Problem: flat window tops would let rain pool and eventually leak. Approach: looked at how slate itself sheds water — stacked and angled, never flat. Result: sloped each window's top edge and recessed the glass 10mm so rain runs straight off.

A fire exit that doesn't wreck the façade

Problem: needed a safe emergency escape route without breaking up the slender stacked-window elevation. Approach: looked at how the existing external staircase could double as an escape path. Result: a recessed exit tunnel tucked behind the stairwell wall, invisible from outside, connecting straight to the external stairs.

The modular floor

The same logic — flexibility under pressure — runs through the modular floor. Steel containers store the roll-up walls and pegboard partitions; a lift brings components up from the third floor so residents can reconfigure the space themselves. Depending on how it's set, the floor splits into four equal bedrooms, two larger suites, or one open performance room.
